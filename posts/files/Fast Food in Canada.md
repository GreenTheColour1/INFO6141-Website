<br/>

## An Overview

<br/>

Fast food has become an important part of daily life in Canada, driven by convenience, busy lifestyles, and the continued expansion of the food service industry.

<br/>

## Current Trend of Canadian Dietary Pattern

<br/>

According to Statistics Canada (2024), fast-food / limited-service restaurants generated **$44.8 billion** in sales in 2024, an increase of $2.3 billion compared with 2023, showing the quick-service restaurants in Canada are growing rapidly. This trend suggests that fast-food culture is economically significant and widely consumed in Canada.

<br/>

## Significant Findings from Recent Studies

<br/>

Research from Liu et al. (2020) also shows that the surrounding food environment plays a significant role in shaping eating behaviors. The findings prove that young adults who are exposed to a greater number of fast-food outlets in their daily activity spaces tend to consume fast food more frequently. When the number of nearby fast-food outlets increased, weekly fast-food consumption increased by about **7.8%–13.8%**, indicating that accessibility directly influences dietary choices.

<br/>

More importantly, the concerns about health outcomes related to fast-food consumption are growing. A recent Canadian study reported by Canadian Press (2025) found that preschool children obtain **nearly half of their calories from ultra-processed foods**, the majority of which include fast-food items. It is concerning because early dietary habits can influence long-term health and increase the risk of obesity.

<br/>

Supporting this, Nardocci et al. (2019) found that ultra-processed foods (including fast food) contribute about **45% of daily calorie intake** among Canadian adults, and this high consumption is associated with a **32% higher likelihood of obesity**. Additionally, Hosseini et al. (2021) reported that older adults who frequently consume fast food have **26% higher odds of metabolic syndrome**, a condition associated with increased risk of cardiovascular disease and diabetes.

<br/>

> **Overall**, these findings suggest that fast food is becoming dominant in Canadian culture; its widespread availability and consumption is causing significant negative health outcomes and arising concerns for public health and policy development.
