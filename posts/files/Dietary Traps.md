Sometimes, what may seem like healthy food, may not actually be that good for you. Whether it be advertisers trying to market their product as healthy or some preservatives that you didn't know are negatively affecting you.

<br/>

## Advertising

<br/>

There are lots of food products that are advertised as healthy, however they not always are. Must people would probably say that Subway is a pretty healthy option among fast food. However They recently faced some trouble in Ireland because the Irish Supreme Court ruled that Subways bread was closer to cake than to bread. The EU has statutory limit where the sugar in the bread should be 2% of the flour base. However Subway’s ‘bread’ came in at 10%, a 5 times the EUs limit (Clayton, 2021).

<br/>

This kind of deceptive marketing is a common tactic. Many processed foods are advertised with a "health halo," where they are packed full of vitamins and minerals to make them seem nutritious. This can trick buyers into overestimating the food's actual healthiness, leading them to ignore high levels of sugar, salt, and unhealthy fats (Kroker-Lobos et al., 2022). Think of a sugary breakfast cereal telling you that it's "a good source of Vitamin D" or a fruit-flavored drink packed with sugar but advertised for its "added Vitamin C."

<br/>

### The Evolution of our Taste Buds

<br/>

It's not just about deceptive advertising, these foods can actually change the way we taste foods. This happens with salt, where food manufacturers find themselves in a "salt race". The more salt you eat, the less you taste it, which leads companies to add more and more to make their products tasty (Clayton, 2021). Additionally, if you reduce salt consumption, your taste buds can become more sensitive, and foods you once enjoyed might start to taste too salty.

<br/>

A similar trap exists with sugar. Humans are wired from birth to prefer sweet tastes. The food industry takes full advantage of this by adding sugar to a vast majority of packaged foods, making it difficult to find even items like bread that don't contain sugar, high-fructose corn syrup, or one of at least 60 other names for added sweeteners. This constant exposure keeps our sweet tooth active and makes naturally sweet foods, like fruit, seem less appealing by comparison (Clayton, 2021).

<br/>

## What You Can Do

<br/>

Navigating these traps can be hard, but a few simple changes can make a difference.

<br/>

- **Read the Whole Label, Not Just the Front.** The front of a package is just advertising, but the Nutrition Facts panel and the ingredients list are where the facts are. Look for the "Added Sugars" line and be careful of products where sugar is one of the first few ingredients.
- **Retrain Your Palate.** Your taste buds can change. By slowly reducing your consumption of overly salty and sugary foods, you will become more sensitive to these tastes again. Foods that once seemed bland will become better, and you'll start to appreciate the natural flavors in whole foods.
- **Embrace Spices.** There is more to flavor than just salt and sugar. Using spices is a great way to add flavor to your meals without the negative health effects.
- **Focus on Whole Foods.** The best way to avoid hidden ingredients and advertising traps is to build your diet around whole foods. Fruits, vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains don't have strange ingredient lists.
